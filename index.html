How I learned to Weld & Solder

Throughout my entire childhood I was always involved in STEM based competitions. Sometimes Science Fair, other times Solar Car and MATE ROV, in these it quickly became essential that I knew how to solder. The extent of the teaching I received was telling me not to eat the solder and not to touch the soldering iron tip. After this in depth introduction I was tasked with repairing everything, from loose wires to circuit boards I did it all. Over time, I've learned how to delicately apply solder and how to trouble shoot any errors that may occur.

Welding is a bit of a shorter story. My first experience welding came when my robotics teacher taught me about friction welding. When people hear the word "welding" most people think of combining two metals. However, you can also combine other materials such as plastic. One specific type of plastic that I used commonly was PLA, by spinning it using a dremmel tool I could liquify it and join together PLA parts.

After becoming proficient in this I moved onto welding metals as well. The image on the left is a little deceiving, I am actually using a gas torch to cut through a plate of iron, I chose to show this because the photos are significantly better and because you can't see much when someone is actually welding. I learned the basics of how to do two types of welding, stick and MIG. Both had a steap learning curve and several times I was left with my tip stuck in the metal. After a couple minutes of practice in each I was able to make some clean dimes!

Utilizing 3d Printing & Modeling

3d printing and modeling are two skills that I also learned in school. I've worked with several modeling softwares, but what I gravitate to most is Fusion360. Fusion 360 is a software which is owned by Autodesk and has many quality of life features that make modeling significantly easier, as well as faster, to do.

Another software that I use both personally and in school activities is Ultimaker Cura. Its ease of use with the different types of supports, various features for build plate adhesion, and automated responses that I can't find in other slicers.

Finally we get to the actual printing and uses of 3d technologies. I primarily use an Ender 3 v3 and a Prusa mk4 which can print essentially anything that I want. I have used both of these to design parts for technology competitions as well as for my own personal use. Generally when using printed items you need to maintain an awareness of which direction it was printed on for two reasons. The first being material consumption, often times to make more intricate components supports are needed. If positioned improperly, additional material may be needed which could otherwise have been concerved for future projects. The second concideration is the build direction of the component. Depending on how it is printed the part will sheer more easily in one direction than another.
